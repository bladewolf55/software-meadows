Suggestions

  1. Put estimates on all tasks.
    1. Do a lot of the short tasks (~< 15min). Clear the decks.
    2. Only target about 4 hours of estimates. There are always more tasks after that!
  2. Determine what's really required, important, nice-to-do, etc. (see exercise)
  3. Set expectations. Say "no" to most tasks (including ones you add on the fly), "maybe" to a bunch, and "yes" to only a few.
    1. Then keep your promise.
  4. For any task, be sure there's a clear, actionable next step. Don't rush recording a task, assuming you'll remember the context later.
  5. Impose Work In Progress limits.

Oh, and one last thing...be kind to yourself.

Observations

You're putting yourself in a pickle by agreeing to do everything that comes your way. All your tasks become equal obligations.

There's a conflict between what's good for others and what's good for you.

The step missing from your system(s) is to regularly and ruthlessly "Review and Prune". You wouldn't skip this step with a garden, otherwise the plants would suffer.

Be more metric-driven with your tasks, for now.

Exercise - What Must I Do?

Write down two or three life areas. They could be Work, Home, Self. Or Career, Personal. Or Career, Marriage, Son, Self. Under those areas, write down what's required for each. That is, what must you absolutely do for them?

Don't overthink this, but do be a critical thinker. For example, it's required to put gas in a car if you want to drive it. A very quick list from my own life might be:

Home

  • Pay mortgage each month (must earn $xxxx.xx)
  • Baseline nutritious meals (oatmeal, eggs, bread, ham, etc) (not snacks) (must earn $xxx.xx)

Marriage

  • Say small, loving things to Steph each day

Career

  • Deliver projects on time

The purpose of this is to find your baseline. You've gotta eat, pay the mortgage. Your job has certain requirements. You have to get Jeremy here and there (but not necessarily everywhere he wants to be!).

With your baseline, you can be sure you're meeting those requirements, and you'll have a better idea how much of your time they take, and how much else you can take on without heroics.

Simplistic Process

The hardest and most helpful thing I've improved in the last years is regularly reviewing and pruning my tasks. Here, roughly, is my daily routine. The system needs to work, sure. But the principles need to be enforced.

Principles

  1. Plan each day.
  2. Use tools that make recording/moving tasks quick and easy.
  3. Capture what comes in.
  4. Reduce distractions.
  5. Limit work in progress. No multi-tasking, and no more than about five tasks in front of you. This also limits task-switching.
  6. Weekly review/prune/reset
  7. Backlog > In Process > [ Waiting ] > Done

My Day

So, my usual day (and week):

  1. Get up, take an hour for exercise, shower, breakfast. This is my time.
  2. Plan my day. This takes about ten minutes. I don't do any work before this, no emails, nothing that gets me sucked in.
    1. If I need to, I prune my backlog dump. That's my "I don't know what to do with this" list. I limit it to ten items.
  3. Break my next few hours into one hour chunks, and what I think I'll do in them. I follow a 50-10 timebox. Fifty minutes of focused work, ten minute break.
    1. During the work, if I get distracted, I write down the distraction. I can do it on break, or delete it, or put it in my backlog or some other "later" bucket. Rarely must I do something right then.
  4. Lunch. I take an hour, no client work, this is my time. Walk, play with the dog, read, other tasks/chores.
  5. Another few hours of work.
  6. Dinner

On Sundays, I take at least an hour for my weekly reset. I make sure I'm not disturbed.

  1. Look at my active list, which has my SOON and TODAY. Too many items? Process them: defer, delegate, delete.
  2. Look at my backlog dump. Process items.
  3. Process my bookmarks backlog. These are web pages I thought might be useful. I end up deleting most of these.
  4. Review my high-level plans. I wrote these at the beginning of the year.
  5. Sometimes I do a few tasks.
  6. Decide on next week's general focus. Things like "blog daily", "release web site". This changes week-to-week.

The above can be reduced to one of my mantras: Think > Do > Review

In this case, it's A) Plan the week/day/hour, B) Do something, C) What got done?

My Tools

I try not to get tool-centered any more. I can get lost in the tool instead of its purpose. But a good tool is still important. I my life, I need to handle:

  • Tasks - things I have to do
  • Events - places I have to be at a certain time
  • Reminders - things I have to do at a certain time
  • Projects - require mutliple steps

Google Keep I use one Keep list for my TODAY, WAITING and SOON lists. This forces me to keep those lists short, so I can see everything. I also have a Month list with high-level month and week goals. And my Backlog Dump. The idea is I can add an item quickly.

Time Box doc
I like the Pomodoro technique's suggestion of writing down the time box periods and a place for distractions. This could be on paper, but I use an electronic document I can switch to at any time.

Google Calendar
Of course, this is where I put my events. I make sure to set notifications for my events, and any other notes such as where it is. If I have a question about an event, I want it in the event or easily linked from it.

Steph shares her calendar with me, and vice-versa. We also have a separate "We're Doing This Together" calendar.

Google Doc I keep my I Can't Believe It's So Huge backlog in a Write file. I don't expect to do these things any time soon, but it's there to pull from.

Kanban Board
For projects, including client and personal projects, I often use a Kanban board. It visualizes the Backlog > In Process > Done flow. I've used bulletin boards, white boards, and scads of software. I don't have a recommendation for you, but here's what it looks like. This is for a personal application I'm working on. Cards with "user stories" (tasks) are dragged from left to right.

The Pomodoro Technique is a 25-5 time boxing cycle. That's too short for software development, so I use 50-10. I created a timer here and then bookmarked it. This works for me because I'm at my desk. If you're on the go, you could use your phone's countdown timer.
